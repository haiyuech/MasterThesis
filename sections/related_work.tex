\chapter{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

This section reviews prior research across three domains relevant to this work: model predictive control for autonomous racing (Section~\ref{sec:rw-mpc}), kinodynamics modeling (Section~\ref{sec:rw-kinodynamics}), and vision foundation models for robotics (Section~\ref{sec:rw-vision}).

\section{Model Predictive Control for Autonomous Racing}
\label{sec:rw-mpc}

Model Predictive Control (MPC) provides a principled framework for constrained optimal control through online trajectory optimization over a receding horizon~\cite{MAYNE2000789}. To handle nonlinear stochastic dynamics, sampling-based formulations such as Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI) control have gained wide adoption. MPPI enables gradient-free optimization over nonlinear systems via stochastic optimal control and importance sampling~\cite{williams2017model,williams2018information}. To improve robustness under uncertainty, Tube-MPPI propagates bounded disturbance tubes around nominal trajectories to enhance constraint satisfaction under model mismatch~\cite{williams2018robust}.

For iterative tasks such as autonomous racing, Learning Model Predictive Control (LMPC) exploits data from prior executions to construct terminal safe sets and approximate value functions, ensuring closed-loop stability and iterative performance improvement~\cite{rosolia2017learning}. More recent work has incorporated expressive probabilistic models, such as normalizing flows, to handle chance constraints under uncertainty~\cite{zang2025sit}. In parallel, safety-critical MPC formulations integrate Control Barrier Functions (CBFs) to enforce forward invariance of safe sets, enabling aggressive operation while preserving safety guarantees~\cite{ames2019control,hafner2019learning}.

Beyond structured environments, perception-aware MPC formulations integrate semantic or geometric scene information, such as elevation maps or risk fields, directly into the optimization objective~\cite{zhai2026pa,xiao2022learning}. Latent dynamics MPC approaches instead learn compact representations of high-dimensional observations and perform planning in the latent space~\cite{hafner2019learning}.

MPC-based racing systems, however, remain constrained by the fidelity and computational cost of the underlying dynamics model. Sampling-based methods such as MPPI require repeated rollout of nonlinear dynamics over large candidate sets, introducing latency that becomes critical in high-speed regimes near friction limits. This limitation motivates the development of more efficient and adaptive dynamics models.

\section{Kinodynamics Modeling}
\label{sec:rw-kinodynamics}

Traditional vehicle dynamics models are derived from physics-based analytical formulations. Common representations range from kinematic bicycle models for low-speed scenarios to dynamic single-track and dual-track models that capture lateral load transfer at high speed~\cite{kong2015kinematic, rajamani2006vehicle}. These rigid-body formulations are augmented with empirical tire models to describe contact forces, including steady-state formulations such as the Pacejka and brush models~\cite{pacejka2005tire}, and dynamic bristle models such as LuGre for transient friction behavior~\cite{de1995new}. While physically interpretable, these models rely on assumptions such as stationary road friction and accurate offline calibration, which degrade performance under changing or unmodeled conditions.

Data-driven approaches address these limitations. Early work employed feedforward neural networks to regress state transitions~\cite{spielberg2019neural}. More recent approaches leverage sequence models, including LSTMs, temporal convolutional networks, and Transformers, to capture temporal dependencies in vehicle dynamics~\cite{zhai2025neural,peng2025transformer}. In off-road settings, forward and inverse kinodynamics models have been learned directly from trajectory data~\cite{xiao2021learning,datar2024learning}. These models, however, often suffer from error accumulation over long horizons and degraded generalization under distribution shift.

Hybrid approaches combine physics-based structure with learned components to balance interpretability and expressiveness. Nominal rigid-body models can be augmented with Gaussian Process or neural residual corrections to capture unmodeled dynamics~\cite{kabzan2019learning}, improving data efficiency through physical priors. Such hybrid models, however, rely primarily on proprioceptive inputs and are therefore limited in capturing environment-dependent dynamics that require exteroceptive sensing.

\section{Vision Foundation Models for Robotics}
\label{sec:rw-vision}

Vision foundation models (VFMs) have been adopted in robotics as transferable visual encoders for perception, planning, and policy learning. Representative architectures include contrastive vision-language models such as CLIP~\cite{radford2021learning} and self-supervised visual encoders such as DINO and DINOv2~\cite{caron2021emerging,oquab2023dinov2}. Pretrained visual representations have been integrated into robotic control pipelines, including language-conditioned visuomotor policies and world-model-based planning~\cite{kang2024clip,zhou2024dino}.

Different pretraining objectives induce distinct representation properties. CLIP emphasizes semantic alignment and category-level understanding, supporting language grounding and high-level reasoning. By contrast, DINO-style models produce spatially consistent and geometry-aware features that are better suited for dense correspondence and scene structure estimation. This distinction is particularly relevant for robotics tasks that depend on accurate geometric understanding of the environment.

Integrating high-dimensional visual representations into dynamics models, however, introduces significant computational overhead. In sampling-based control frameworks such as MPPI, the dynamics model is evaluated repeatedly across many candidate rollouts. Propagating visual features at each rollout step substantially reduces control frequency, limiting the applicability of vision-conditioned models in real-time systems.
